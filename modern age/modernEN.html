End of an age

The death of Queen Victoria, who had been a symbol of the values, the greatness and the weakness of the age, marked a turning point. On the Queen's death , her son Edward VII came to the throne(1901-1910).

His age can be seen as a period of social changes. Important milestones in this process were the Education Act, which guaranteed secondary education, and legislation that recognized the right of Trade Union to strike.

Old age pensions and National Health Insurance were introduced and government offices, where people went to find jobs, were opened.

Britain's foreign policy

In the last decades of the 19th century, England's foreign policy had been one of 'splendid isolation'. Europe was then divided between France and Russia on one side, Germany, Austria and Italy on the other.

After the turn of the century, however, Britain became concerned about the expansion of the German Empire. In 1904, Britain and France, both frightened by Germany, signed a treaty of friendship. In 1907, this agreement became the Triple Entente, when France's ally, Russia, joined.

It was opposed by the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria and Italy. On Edward VII's death, his son George V(1910-1936) came to the throne. His reign is often associated with the horrors of the First World War (1914-1918) and the sense of disillusionment that followed the conflict.

The First World War

World War I, was a global war that began on 28 July 1914 and ended on 11 November 1918. It involved much of Europe, as well as Russia, the United States and Turkey, and was also fought in the Middle East, Africa and parts of Asia. One of the deadliest conflicts in history, an estimated 9 million were killed in combat, while over 5 million civilians died from occupation, bombardment, hunger or disease. Genocides and the 1918 Spanish flu pandemic spread by the movement of combatants during the war caused many millions of additional deaths worldwide.

On Edward VII's death, his son George V(1910-1936) came to the throne. His reign is often associated with the horrors of the First World War (1914-1918) and the sense of disillusionment that followed the conflict. Tensions in the Balkans came to a head on 28 June 1914 following the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, the Austro-Hungarian heir, by Gavrilo Princip, a Bosnian Serb. Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia and the interlocking alliances involved the Powers in a series of diplomatic exchanges known as the July Crisis.

On 28 July, Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia; Russia came to Serbia's defence and by 4 August, the conflict had expanded to include Germany, France and Britain, along with their respective colonial empires. In November, the Ottoman Empire, Germany and Austria formed the Central Powers, while in April 1915, Italy joined Britain, France, Russia and Serbia as the Allied Powers.

Post-war years

The human cost of the First World War was horrendous. More than 16 million people, both military and civilian, died in the war. An entire generation of young men was wiped away. In 1919, the year after the war was over in France, there were 15 women for every man between the ages of 18 and 30. It is tragic to consider all of the lost potential, all of the writers, artists, teachers, inventors and leaders that were killed in ‘the war to end all wars.’

All attempts at reconstruction seemed definitely shattered with the Great Depression in 1929. The Great Depression was a severe worldwide economic depression that took place mostly during the 1930s, beginning in the United States. The timing of the Great Depression varied around the world; in most countries, it started in 1929 and lasted until the late 1930s. The Great Depression had devastating effects in both rich and poor countries. Personal income, tax revenue, profits and prices dropped, while international trade fell by more than 50%. Unemployment in the U.S. rose to 23% and in some countries rose as high as 33%.

But although the impact of the First World War was hugely destructive it also produced many new developments in medicine, warfare, politics and social attitudes. The First World War changed the nature of warfare. Technology became an essential element in the art of war with airplanes, submarines, tanks all playing important new roles. Modern surgery was born in the First World War, where civil and military hospitals acted as theatres of experimental medical intervention.

The British Commonwealth of Nations

The Commonwealth of Nations, generally known simply as the Commonwealth,[3] is a political association of 54 member states, almost all of which are former territories of the British Empire. The chief institutions of the organisation are the Commonwealth Secretariat, which focuses on intergovernmental aspects, and the Commonwealth Foundation, which focuses on non-governmental relations amongst member states.

The Commonwealth dates back to the first half of the 20th century with the decolonisation of the British Empire through increased self-governance of its territories. It was originally created as the British Commonwealth of Nations through the Balfour Declaration at the 1926 Imperial Conference, and formalised by the United Kingdom through the Statute of Westminster in 1931. The current Commonwealth of Nations was formally constituted by the London Declaration in 1949, which modernised the community and established the member states as "free and equal".

Many Commonwealth nations possess traditions and customs that are elements of a shared Commonwealth culture. Examples include common sports such as cricket and rugby, driving on the left, the Westminster system of parliamentary democracy, common law, widespread use of the English language, designation of English as an official language, military and naval ranks, and the use of British rather than American spelling conventions (see English in the Commonwealth of Nations).

The year of the three kings

The year of George V's death(1936) is known as the year of the Three Kings. The succession of George's son to the throne as Edward VIII was very short.

He abdicated and his brother George VI came to the throne. He reigned well and wisely having to face the problems that arose out of Britain's involvement

in the Second World War. His daughter, Elizabeth became Queen of England in 1952.

The rise of dictatorship

Dictatorships emerged during the 1920s and 1930s. Totalitarian governments found fertile ground for growth in Russia, Italy, Germany and other countries. In Russia, Communism came to power in the revolution of November 1917. In Italy, Mussolini founded the Fascist party in 1919.

He quickly seized all powers of government and transformed the country into a totalitarian state. In Germany, the National Socialist, or Nazi, party came to power in 1933.

Adolf Hitler crushed all political opposition and started to rebuild Germany's armed strength. In Japan, by 1931, the militarists began to dominate the government.

The Second World War(1939-1945)

The three main causes of the Second World War were:
- the problems left unsolved after WW1;
- the rise of dictatorship;
- the desire of Germany,Italy and Japan for more territory.

The war saw the Allied powers(France and Britain, and later Russia and the USA) against the Axis powers(Germany,Italy,Japan). In England the Conservative government was replaced by a coalition headed by Winston Churchill(1874-1965). His inspired leadership united the people if Britain against Nazi Germany and its allies.

He negotiated the alliance with the USSR and the USA and met Stalin and Roosevelt at three major conferences(in Teheran, 1943; in Yalta and Potsdam, both in 1945), in which the allies coordinated the major campaigns of the war and then discussed post-war settlements, by which the world was divided into 'Zones of Influence'.
