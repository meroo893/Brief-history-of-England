Progressive dissolution of the British Empire

The accession to the throne of Elizabeth II(1952) made some anticipate a new 'Elizabethan Era', owing to the growing prosperity of the country and the general well-being of the population. In the general euphoria there was a revival of nationalism and even of imprerialism. Dissolution soon came, though.

The post-war years witnessed the progressive dissolution of the British Empire. After the concession of independence to India and Pakistan(1947) almost all the former colonies became self-governing communities within the association formed by the Statue of Westminster(1931), known as the Commonwealth.

The transition did not always take place smoothly and in some cases it left unsolved situations behind but, altogether, the liquidation of the Empire was accomplished without violence.

European integration

The Conservative government (1959-1963) with the intention to counteract the unsteady economic growth started negotiations on entry into EEC(European Economic Community). Britain first joined EFTA(European Free Trade Association) and then, in 1961, formally applied for membership of the Common Market.

However, the French leader, Charles De Gaulle, in 1963 vetoed British entry into the EEC. De Gaulle, who never believed in a politically united Europe, was resolved ot build up a Europe of the homelands that would become a third superpower, between the USA and the USSR, and was suspicious of Britain's close bonds with Washington.

Only the resignation of De Gaulle, in 1969, opened up the possibility of British entry. Britain finnaly entered EEC in January 1973. However, while subscribing to many EU policies, the UK chose to maintain its own currency instead of the euro.

The Welfare State

The post-way for years, the newly elected Labour government set about creating the Welfare State. This was a system of national insurance crated to protect everyone from 'the cradle to the grave'. The expression 'Welfare State' essentially means that totality of plans and services through which central Government together with local authorities assumes a major responsibility for dealing with all the different types of social problems.

This was first and foremost designed to maintain a high level of employment through the nationalisation of major industries. Another air of the Welfare State was to provide efficient public services, particularly education, health and transport. To pay for social security and services the government relied on taxation.

Up until 1979, succesive governments dealt with the problem of how to maintain a tolerable distribution of wealth and services, and with a series of government acts reconised the need for the true equality between sexes. The Equal Pay Act(1970) and the Sex Discrimination Act(1975) made discriminatory treatment of women in the workplace illegal. Besides, the Race Realtions Act of 1976 tried to halt the racist tendencies emerging in an ingreasingly multiethnic society.

The Iron Lady and Conservative domination

In 1979, the British electorate voted in a Conservative government, led by Margaret Thatcher (1979-1990), the first woman to be a leader of a political party in Britain and the first woman Prime Minister.

M 'The Iron Lady' as she came to be known, led a crusade against the Welfare State. She embodied a reaction against what she called the 'culture of dependancy'.

She is associated with a return to the spirit of Victorian free enterprise. It is generally agreed that from Thatcher on both Cnservative and New Labour governments have undermined the Welfare State. The Conservatives aggresively, New Labour less so.

Brexit

The three recent British PMs are generally associated with the process of withdrawal of the UK from the EU, caller Brexit. Britain has been debatins the pros and cons of membership in a European community of nations almost from the moment the idea was broached.

It held its first referendum on membership in what was then called European Economic Community in 1975, less than three years after it joined. But that was hardly the end of the debate.

In 2013, PM David Cameron promised a national referendum on EU membership with the idea of settling the question once and for all. Following a UK-wide referendum in June 2016, the British government formally announced the country's withdrawal in March 2017, beggining the Brexit process.
